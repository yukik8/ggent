# US Consumer Healthcare Access Stat Pack 2026

**Source:** Meridian & Craft research desk syndication of the Callowhill Health Access Survey, supplemented with appointment-flow data licensed from ClinicPulse Benchmarks.

**Methodology:** A nationally weighted survey of 18,500 US adults fielded September through December 2025, combined with scheduling and billing records from 3,100 primary-care and urgent-care sites across 42 metro areas.

## Access Overview

Access to routine care in the US is fragmenting along generational lines. 41% of US adults under 45 have no primary-care physician, up from 33% in 2019. The shift is not primarily driven by cost or insurance status. Among the unattached under-45 cohort, roughly two thirds carry commercial coverage, and most describe themselves as "healthy enough to wait." What they are actually doing is routing episodic needs to urgent care, retail clinics, and telehealth, treating the traditional PCP relationship as optional.

## The Scheduling Bottleneck

Even patients who want a regular doctor hit friction at the front door. The average wait for a new-patient primary-care appointment in mid-size metros is 26 days. Waits in the largest ten metros run somewhat shorter thanks to denser provider supply, while rural counties in the panel stretched past five weeks. Scheduling delay was the single most cited reason survey respondents abandoned an attempted new-patient registration, ahead of insurance confusion and location.

## How Younger Patients Choose

Clinic selection behavior has inverted for the under-40 cohort. 64% of patients under 40 choose a clinic based on online booking availability before considering reputation. Reviews and word of mouth still matter, but they function as a tiebreaker after the booking test is passed, not as the entry filter. Clinics in the panel without any self-serve scheduling captured a disproportionately older patient mix, regardless of clinical quality scores.

## Pricing Transparency as a Growth Lever

Price opacity is no longer just a compliance issue, it is a conversion issue. Transparent cash-pay pricing pages increase new-patient bookings by 34% for urgent-care services. The effect held across income bands and was strongest among the uninsured and high-deductible populations. Sites that published a simple flat-rate visit menu outperformed those that posted full chargemaster-style tables, suggesting clarity matters more than completeness.

## Retention After the First Visit

Urgent care is often framed as inherently transactional, but the panel data says otherwise. 55% of urgent-care visitors return to the same network within 18 months when they receive a follow-up message within a week. Without any follow-up contact, return rates fell to roughly a third. The content of the message mattered less than its timing, a plain-text check-in performed comparably to designed campaigns.

## Outlook

Expect the unattached-patient share to keep climbing through 2027 as under-45 consumers continue defaulting to on-demand channels. Networks that win this cohort will be those that treat online booking, posted pricing, and fast post-visit follow-up as core clinical infrastructure rather than marketing add-ons.
