# US Home Improvement Retail Stat Pack 2026

**Source:** Meridian & Craft research desk syndication of the Beacon Hardline Retail annual tracker, supplemented with foot-traffic panel data licensed from DoorCount Analytics.

**Methodology:** Point-of-sale data from 9,400 US home improvement and hardware outlets combined with a longitudinal survey panel of 18,500 homeowners, fielded January through December 2025.

## Category Overview

Home improvement retail closed a strong year, though the growth story is more about who is buying than how much. US home improvement retail reached $538B in 2025, with DIY purchases accounting for 52% of revenue. The DIY share is notable because it crossed the halfway mark for the first time since the tracker began, driven largely by younger homeowners taking on projects that previous cohorts would have hired out.

## The Confidence Gap

The biggest untapped opportunity in the category is not price sensitivity but hesitation. 69% of first-time homeowners say they feel intimidated starting any project beyond painting. In follow-up interviews, panelists cited fear of "making it worse," not knowing which tools to buy, and past experiences of abandoning half-finished projects. Retailers that treat this hesitation as a merchandising problem consistently underperform those that treat it as an education problem.

## Content That Converts

The tracker's media-effectiveness module found a clear winner among marketing formats. Project-based how-to content drives 2.7x more store visits than price-and-item advertising for regional chains. A circular listing a drill at a discount moves inventory; a video showing a nervous homeowner installing their first shelf moves people into the store. The effect was strongest among the under-40 cohort, where price-and-item ads barely registered above baseline.

## Workshops and Wallet Share

In-person education pays off well beyond the event itself. In-store workshop attendees spend 43% more annually than matched non-attendees. Attendees also return sooner after their first visit and are roughly twice as likely to purchase in adjacent categories such as safety gear and finishing supplies. The panel suggests workshops work less as a sales channel and more as a confidence-building mechanism that unlocks the hesitant majority described above.

## Fulfillment and Retention

How customers receive their orders shapes whether they come back. Regional hardware chains retain 71% of customers who use in-store pickup, versus 48% of delivery-only customers. Pickup customers interact with staff, encounter project displays, and often add items at the counter. Delivery-only customers, by contrast, behave like commodity shoppers and churn to whichever channel is cheapest that month.

## Outlook

Expect the DIY share of revenue to hold above half through 2026 as first-time homeowners age into more ambitious projects. Regional chains that pair how-to content with in-store workshops and steer fulfillment toward pickup are positioned to convert the intimidated 69% into repeat project buyers, while chains competing on price and delivery alone will keep bleeding retention.
