## Opening: The Problem We Are Actually Solving

Hartline's category has a confidence problem, and Hartline owns the best tool for fixing it. 69% of first-time homeowners say they feel intimidated starting any project beyond painting (ref-14) — they save project ideas constantly, watch tutorials repeatedly, and still don't start, because they are afraid of making it worse. That hesitation is the single largest pool of unconverted demand in a market where US home improvement retail reached $538B in 2025, with DIY purchases accounting for 52% of revenue (ref-14).

The workshop program is our answer to that specific problem, and the economics say it is worth relaunching properly: in-store workshop attendees spend 43% more annually than matched non-attendees (ref-14). A free workshop is the one place a hesitant homeowner can practice on someone else's drywall before risking their own — and the $49-$149 take-home project kits convert that borrowed confidence into a first purchase the same day. The relaunch runs one flagship workshop per store per month across all 40 stores: fall fix-ups in September and October, holiday prep in November and December, and a "New Year, New Skills" series in January. Every tactic below exists to fill workshop seats with intimidated homeowners and send them home mid-project.

## Target Audience

DIY-curious homeowners aged 28-50 in Hartline's trade areas — the audience living inside the 69% intimidation statistic (ref-14). They save project ideas constantly but hesitate to start; the workshop is built for exactly that gap between saving and doing. Secondary: existing DIYers leveling up to new skill categories.

## The Tactic Stack

Because the core problem is hesitation, every tactic is judged on one question: does it move an intimidated homeowner closer to a seat in a workshop?

### 1. Workshop Livestreams
One flagship workshop per month streams on TikTok Live and Instagram Live. Because the core problem is fear of starting, watching a real, imperfect session — mistakes included — lowers the barrier for people not yet ready to attend, and gives every store's program reach beyond the room.

### 2. Short-Form Recap Content
Every workshop generates recap Reels and TikToks. This is guidance-led media, not price-led media, and that distinction is measured: project-based how-to content drives 2.7x more store visits than price-and-item advertising for regional chains (ref-14). Recaps are the campaign's awareness engine because they show ordinary people finishing projects.

### 3. UGC Hashtag Challenge
Attendees post finished projects under #HartlineHandsOn. A hesitant homeowner trusts a neighbor's wobbly-but-standing shelf more than any brand ad; the hashtag turns every graduate into proof that the fear is beatable.

### 4. Local Creator Takeovers
DIY and home creators from each metro co-host one workshop per quarter. The format is also efficient media: creator-partnership posts deliver 31% lower cost-per-engagement than brand-owned posts (ref-06), and takeover sessions feed the recap and livestream pipeline.

### 5. Email & CRM Push
Workshop invitations are lifecycle moments, not blasts, and the channel rewards that: lifecycle-triggered emails average a 38% open rate, versus 21% for standard campaign sends (ref-16). Monthly calendars go to the full list, with reminder flows for registrants and follow-up offers on the matching project kits.

### 6. In-Store Signage & Local PR
Permanent workshop zones in all 40 stores plus local news and community-calendar pitches keep the program visible in the small-market media that Hartline's trade areas actually read.

## Why Workshops, Why Now

Because the named problem is intimidation, the workshop is not an event tactic — it is the conversion mechanism for the hesitant majority. Attendance is free to keep the barrier at zero, the $49-$149 kits give every session a natural register moment, and the 43% annual spending premium among attendees (ref-14) means each filled seat compounds well beyond the campaign window.
