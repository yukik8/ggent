## Opening: The Problem We Are Actually Solving

Stackwise's growth ceiling is not awareness of budgeting apps; it is that young earners try them and quit. 58% of US adults under 35 used a budgeting or savings app in 2025, up from 44% in 2022 (ref-13) — the category is mainstream — yet the average consumer fintech app loses 73% of new users within 30 days of download (ref-13). For first-job earners aged 20-27 with no savings habit, the pattern is predictable: they download out of guilt, face an empty dashboard, and churn before anything changes.

The First Stack Challenge is our answer to that specific problem. A guided 90-day program — auto-moving small amounts toward a default $500 "first stack," with milestone celebrations along the way — gives a new user a reason to still be here on day 30, day 60, and day 90. The free tier carries the full challenge; the $8/month premium tier adds custom buckets and advanced rules. Every tactic below is selected to convert a guilty download into a 90-day habit, not just to generate installs.

## The Big Idea

**"Your first $500 is the hardest. Let's make it the easiest."**

Because the core problem is that saving fails as a private struggle, the challenge makes it a shared, structured one. The framing is deliberate: only 22% of this cohort responds to discount-led messaging as a first touch, while 49% respond to problem-solution storytelling (ref-07). The story we tell is the problem itself — no savings, first job, quiet panic — resolved in 90 visible days.

---

## Target Audience

First-job earners aged 20-27 with no savings habit. Two measured traits shape every creative decision. First, they distrust hype: 61% of savers under 30 say they distrust finance apps that lead with investment returns instead of safety (ref-13), so the challenge never mentions returns, yield, or investing — only a locked bucket and a number that grows. Second, they demand proof: 58% of Gen-Z respondents say they distrust ads that make claims without visible evidence or numbers (ref-07), so every asset shows real progress screens with real, modest amounts.

## Channel Plan

Because the core problem is habit formation, each channel is assigned to a stage of it: discover, believe, join, persist.

### 1. Paid Social — Short-Form Video
Discovery happens in the feed: 71% of Gen-Z respondents discover new brands primarily through short-form video (ref-07). It is also the efficient buy — short-form video app-install campaigns average a $4.10 CPI for finance apps, versus $6.80 on search (ref-13), and short-form video ads average 2.4x the completion rate of static feed ads (ref-06). The spots show progress screens filling and day-90 reveals.

### 2. Creator Partnerships — "My First Stack" Series
Belief requires witnesses, not spokespeople. 64% of Gen-Z respondents have purchased a product recommended by a niche creator with under 100K followers (ref-07), so our roster is relatable first-job creators, not finance gurus, documenting their own 90 days. The format is also efficient: creator-partnership posts deliver 31% lower cost-per-engagement than brand-owned posts (ref-06).

### 3. Challenge Hub & Landing Experience
Joining must survive scrutiny — Gen-Z respondents check an average of 2.7 sources before a first purchase over $50 (ref-07), and they apply the same diligence to linking a bank account. The hub answers every trust question in seconds, shows live community totals, and routes to download.

### 4. Lifecycle Email & Push
Persistence is carried by lifecycle messaging, the channel built for it: lifecycle-triggered emails average a 38% open rate, versus 21% for standard campaign sends (ref-16). Milestone celebrations, gentle re-engagement when a week goes quiet, and the day-90 moment with its premium trial offer.

## Why This Will Work

The category's adoption curve is rising while its retention floor stays broken, and the winner will be the app that fixes day 30, not the one that shouts loudest at day 0. The First Stack Challenge attacks the churn problem with structure, the trust problem with safety-first framing and visible numbers, and the discovery problem with the channels this cohort actually uses — each tactic answerable to the same named problem.
