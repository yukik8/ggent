## Campaign Title

---

## Opening: The Problem We Are Actually Solving

Sunbrook's problem this fall is not the occasion; it is who gets remembered when the host writes the shopping list. Only 27% of snack buyers aged 18-34 can name a preferred mid-market crisp brand unprompted (ref-01), and private-label share of salty snacks rose from 18% to 23% between 2021 and 2024 (ref-01) — nowhere more aggressively than in party-quantity formats, where the store-brand multipack sits a dollar cheaper. The loyalty gap compounds it: repeat purchase rate for the average mid-market snack brand is 31%, versus 52% for the top-3 national brands (ref-01).

Game Day Batch is our answer to that specific problem. A season-long weekly occasion means the same host buys eight to ten times between September and November — a repeat-purchase machine if Sunbrook becomes the ritual. Two limited flavors (Stadium BBQ and Cheese Blitz), a 6-count multipack at $8.99 with a serving-tray box, and $4.29 single bags give the host a reason to reach past private label every single week. The category can fund the push: US salty snacks retail was valued at $34.2B in 2024, growing 3.1% YoY (ref-01). Every tactic below is selected to move unaided recall and weekly repeat, not one-off trial.

---

## Target Audience

| Segment | Description |
|---|---|
| Primary | Football watch-party hosts aged 21-44 in Midwest drive markets |
| Secondary | Guests who taste the product at a party and buy later |

Hosts plan gatherings on the platforms where brand discovery happens — 71% of Gen-Z respondents discover new brands primarily through short-form video (ref-07) — and they respond to usefulness over promotion: only 22% respond to discount-led messaging as a first touch while 49% respond to problem-solution storytelling (ref-07). Our creative therefore leads with hosting solutions, never with a coupon.

---

## The Big Idea

**"Bring the Batch"** makes Sunbrook the official move for whoever hosts. Because the core problem is unaided recall against a cheaper private label, the weekly rituals (the pre-game stack, the flavor draft) matter strategically: they attach the Sunbrook name to a repeating occasion, attacking the 31%-versus-52% repeat gap (ref-01) one game week at a time.

## Channel & Media Plan

| Channel | Role |
|---|---|
| Paid social — short-form video (Reels, TikTok) | Weekly game-day content flights timed to matchups; hosting hacks and flavor-draft formats |
| Creator partnerships | Food and tailgate creators run "Bring the Batch" hosting content through the season |
| Grocery retail media | Sponsored placements on grocery partner networks against snack and party searches |
| In-store sampling & displays | Weekend sampling with team-table end-caps in priority Midwest markets |

Because the recall gap lives in short-form feeds, paid social carries the weekly drumbeat at efficient cost — Instagram Reels average a $9.40 CPM and TikTok $7.80 (ref-06) — with frequency capped, since frequency above 4 exposures per week correlates with an 18% drop in engagement rate (ref-06). Creators make the ritual credible: 64% of Gen-Z respondents have purchased a product recommended by a niche creator with under 100K followers (ref-07), and creator-partnership posts deliver 31% lower cost-per-engagement than brand-owned posts (ref-06). Retail media defends the weekly shop, where grocery campaigns average a ROAS of 3.8x for snack and beverage brands and on-site sponsored placements drive a 15% average lift in units per store per week (ref-08). And because in-store sampling lifts trial conversion by 19% on average in grocery channels (ref-01), the team-table end-caps convert hosts at shelf and start the weekly loop.
