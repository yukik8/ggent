# Persona: Nate, the No-Doctor Guy

**Client:** Clearbrook Health (c-12, consumer healthcare)

## Snapshot
- **Name:** Nate Ferris
- **Age:** 36
- **Occupation:** Field operations manager for a fiber-internet installer, on the road between job sites most weekdays
- **Location:** Boise, Idaho suburbs, married with a 4-year-old daughter

## Who he is
Nate hasn't had a primary-care physician since he aged off his parents' pediatrician, and he doesn't feel bad about it because half the guys on his crew are the same. He's on a high-deductible plan through work, so in his head every visit is out-of-pocket anyway, and a bill of unknown size is scarier than the symptom. He runs his whole life through his phone, mortgage, daycare payments, oil changes, and it genuinely baffles him that most clinics still want him to call during business hours. Healthcare only enters his world when something breaks, a wrist he jammed coaching tee-ball, a sinus thing that won't quit, his daughter spiking a fever on a Saturday night. His wife handles their daughter's pediatrician; Nate himself is the household's unmanaged risk, and at 36 he's starting to quietly suspect it.

## Media diet
- YouTube in the truck cab at lunch, mostly home-gym, BBQ, and DIY channels, plus podcasts on the drive between sites
- Instagram and a little Reddit (r/Boise, r/personalfinance) in the evening, skips anything that smells like an ad in the first two seconds
- Google Maps and search reviews are his real decision engine, he reads the three most recent reviews and checks whether he can book from the listing
- Ignores mailers and TV spots, but a billboard with an actual price on it will stick in his head

## Buying triggers
- A flat, printed price, $149 for a visit is a number he can weigh against toughing it out, "we accept most insurance" tells him nothing
- Being able to book a same-day slot from his phone at 9pm without talking to anyone, if there's a phone-only step he bails
- Weekend and evening hours, because a weekday appointment means burning PTO he's saving for family trips
- His daughter needing care when the pediatrician is closed, the fastest way he ever tries a new clinic
- A text follow-up after a visit that makes the next step one tap, book the recheck, book the flu shot, done

## Frustrations
- Calling a primary-care office and hearing the first new-patient opening is a month out, he took that deal once, felt insulted, never rescheduled
- Getting a surprise bill weeks after a visit that's triple what he guessed, this is the story he tells when anyone asks why he avoids doctors
- Waiting rooms with two-hour drift and no queue visibility, he can watch his food delivery move on a map but not his own appointment
- Paperwork on a clipboard asking for information he already typed into the website
- Feeling lectured about not having a doctor by the very system that made getting one take a month

## What becoming a regular looks like
Nate's first Clearbrook visit will almost certainly be an urgent-care walk-in he booked from the couch. Whether there's ever a second one depends entirely on the week after, if a follow-up text lands while the visit is still fresh, he'll tap it, and once he's been twice the $79/month membership stops sounding like a subscription and starts sounding like finally having "a guy" without the 26-day wait. If nobody follows up, Clearbrook becomes one more place he went once.

## Quote
"I'm not scared of doctors, I'm scared of the bill and the phone call. Show me the price, let me book it like a haircut, and I'm there."
