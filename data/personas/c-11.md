# Persona: Rachel, the Intimidated New Homeowner

**Client:** Hartline Home & Hardware (c-11, home improvement retail)

## Snapshot
- **Name:** Rachel Beaumont
- **Age:** 33
- **Occupation:** Pediatric nurse at a Tulsa hospital, three 12-hour shifts a week
- **Location:** Broken Arrow, Oklahoma, a 1998 three-bedroom she and her husband closed on eleven months ago

## Who she is
Rachel spent her twenties renting, where every problem was a phone call to the landlord. Now the house is hers and every squeaky door, ugly light fixture, and patchy lawn feels like a quiet accusation. She has a Pinterest board called "Someday House" with 240 pins and a garage containing exactly one hammer, a tape measure, and the Allen keys that came with the furniture. She painted the guest room last spring and was proud of it for weeks, but anything past painting feels like a wall she can't see over. She isn't looking for products so much as permission, someone to tell her the towel-bar project won't end with a hole in the drywall and a $300 repair bill.

## Media diet
- YouTube how-to videos on days off, watches three tutorials before touching anything, then often doesn't touch anything
- Pinterest and Instagram for the "after" photos, saves constantly, acts rarely
- A local Facebook group for her subdivision, where neighbors recommend contractors and stores by name
- Skips price-and-item flyers entirely, a "$4 off drill bits" ad means nothing to someone who doesn't own a drill

## Buying triggers
- Content that walks a whole project start to finish, tools, steps, mistakes to avoid, then points to one shopping list she can grab in a single trip
- A starter bundle in the $79-$249 range that removes the "which of these 14 caulk guns" decision entirely
- A free in-store workshop where she can practice on someone else's drywall before risking her own
- An employee who asks what she's working on instead of where she wants to be pointed, this is why she drives past a big-box store to reach Hartline
- Buy-online-pickup-in-store, so she can order after a night shift and grab it without wandering aisles feeling lost

## Frustrations
- Big-box stores where the aisle signage assumes she already knows what a "toggle anchor" is and staff are impossible to find
- Tutorials that say "simply" before a step that is not simple
- Buying the wrong part, driving back, and being made to feel dumb at the return counter
- Project cost creep, the $40 Pinterest project that quietly requires $150 of tools she'll use once
- Her husband deferring every fix to "a guy we should call," which doubles the cost and the wait

## What her first real project looks like
Rachel's breakthrough purchase won't be a tool, it will be a finished thing, a mounted shelf that didn't fall down. If a Hartline workshop or starter kit gets her through one project cleanly, she converts fast, she comes back with the next pin from the board, asks for the same associate by name, and tells the subdivision Facebook group exactly which store fixed her fear. Lose her once with a wrong-part trip and she retreats to hiring it out for a year.

## Quote
"I don't need the cheapest drill. I need someone to promise me I'm not going to hit a pipe."
