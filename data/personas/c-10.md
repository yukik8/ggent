# Persona: Diego, the Paycheck-to-Paycheck Starter

**Client:** Stackwise (c-10, consumer fintech)

## Snapshot
- **Name:** Diego Fuentes
- **Age:** 24
- **Occupation:** Tier-1 customer support rep at a telecom contractor, $41k salary, biweekly direct deposit
- **Location:** San Antonio, Texas, splits a two-bedroom apartment with a roommate

## Who he is
Diego is eighteen months into his first real job and still hasn't figured out where the money goes. Rent, his car payment, and his phone bill hit early in the month, and by the second paycheck he's rationing DoorDash and telling friends "next weekend." He wants savings, not to get rich, just a cushion so a flat tire isn't a crisis, but every attempt at a spreadsheet died within two weeks. He's downloaded two budgeting apps before and deleted both inside a month, one because it kept pushing him toward crypto and stock picks, which set off every alarm he has. He sends a little money to his mom when he can, which he never mentions but always budgets around first.

## Media diet
- TikTok and Instagram Reels, 90+ minutes daily, mostly gaming clips, cooking, and the occasional "how I saved $5k" video he watches skeptically
- YouTube on the TV at night, lets autoplay run
- Group chats are his real news feed, an app has to survive being screenshotted and roasted there
- Skips or distrusts anything that looks like a finance-bro ad, Lambos, screenshots of gains, "passive income" language all read as scams to him

## Buying triggers
- "Free to start" with the price of premium stated plainly up front, $8/month feels honest, hidden pricing feels like a trap
- Safety-first framing, FDIC-style reassurance, "your money stays yours," no investment pitch anywhere near the signup flow
- Seeing a real person his age on short-form video show their actual app screen with an unimpressive but real number, like $340 saved
- A setup that takes under five minutes, if linking his paycheck feels like a mortgage application he bails

## Frustrations
- Apps that gate the one useful feature behind a paywall he discovers on day three
- Anything that leads with investing or returns, he's under 30 and broke, talk of yield makes him assume he's the product
- Budgeting tools that make him feel judged, red warnings and "overspent!" badges just make him close the app
- Notification spam, two useless pings and the app gets muted, muted apps get deleted

## What sticking with the app looks like
Diego is exactly the user most apps lose in the first month. The version of him that stays is the one who linked his payroll deposit in week one, watched $25 auto-move somewhere he couldn't accidentally spend it, and got one quiet notification a week telling him the number went up. Three months in he's at a few hundred dollars for the first time in his life, shows his roommate the screen unprompted, and starts wondering if the $8/month tier is worth it for the extra buckets.

## Quote
"I don't want to invest, man, I want to stop being scared of my own bank app. Just show me I've got a little something put away."
