# Persona: Greg, the Reformed Gym-Quitter

**Client:** Kinetiq Home (c-08, connected fitness)

## Snapshot
- **Name:** Greg Tallman
- **Age:** 38
- **Occupation:** Regional operations manager for a building-supplies distributor, mostly WFH with two site visits a week
- **Location:** Naperville, Illinois suburbs, two kids (6 and 9), finished basement with one sad set of dumbbells

## Who he is
Greg has quit two gyms in six years, and he can tell you the exact month each membership turned into a donation. It was never laziness, it was logistics, by the time the kids were fed and the inbox was cleared, the 25-minute round trip to the gym had lost to the couch. His doctor flagged his blood pressure at his last physical, and his brother-in-law just got a rowing machine he will not shut up about. Greg wants a machine at home, but he has a specific nightmare, spending four figures on something that becomes the world's most expensive clothes rack by March. He is not looking to become an athlete, he is looking for 30 minutes, four times a week, that actually happens.

## Media diet
- YouTube in the evenings, gravitates to honest "6 months later" equipment reviews over launch-day hype
- Reddit lurker on r/homegym and r/Rowing, reads the "did you regret it" threads closely
- Podcasts during site-visit drives, mostly business and sports, notices host-read ads more than pre-rolls
- Facebook mostly for the neighborhood group and Marketplace, where he sees rows of barely-used exercise bikes listed for half price, which feeds the fear
- Skips anything with a shredded 25-year-old in it, trusts testimonials from people who look like him

## Buying triggers
- A free in-home trial or a real return window, being able to test it in his own basement before the money is truly gone is the single biggest unlock
- Proof of habit, not proof of fitness, retention numbers, streak features, anything showing that normal people are still rowing a year in
- A "6 months later" review from a fellow ex-gym-quitter who stuck with it
- Spousal sign-off math, being able to show his wife the $39/month membership plus hardware still beats two gym memberships they were not using
- His brother-in-law's standing offer to let him try the rower over Thanksgiving

## Frustrations
- Ads that sell the sculpted after-photo instead of the Tuesday-night reality of squeezing in a workout between dinner and bedtime stories
- Being locked into a subscription on top of a big hardware price, it feels like paying twice to maybe fail
- Equipment brands that go quiet after the sale, no onboarding, no plan for week three when motivation dips
- The Facebook Marketplace graveyard of $1,800 bikes for $700, every listing reads like a warning label
- Gym contracts with cancellation mazes, he still resents the certified letter he had to send in 2022

## What sticking with it looks like
Greg's make-or-break window is the first month. If the classes and the calendar get him rowing eight or more times in those first four weeks, the habit locks in and the machine earns permanent basement real estate, he becomes the guy telling his own brother-in-law story at Thanksgiving. If week one is confusing or week two goes silent, the rower starts collecting laundry and he quietly cancels the membership before the second bill.

## Quote
"I don't need it to change my life, I need to still be using it in February. Show me the guy who bought one last year and still rows, and I'm in."
