# Stackwise Credit-Builder Launch Campaign — DRAFT

**Prepared by:** Marcus Bell, Junior Planner, Meridian & Craft
**Client:** Stackwise
**Campaign window:** August 1 – December 31, 2026
**Budget:** $450,000

---

## Campaign Title

**"The Credit Glow-Up" — the Stackwise Credit-Builder Drop**

---

## Opening

Stackwise Credit-Builder is the product's next big swing: a $5/month add-on that reports on-time "rent-yourself" payments to the bureaus and turns a user's existing savings behavior into a growing credit score. No credit check to join, no interest, no card to lose in a pocket. It slots straight into the app people already use every payday, and it gives Stackwise a second monetization lane beyond premium.

Launches like this deserve launch energy. The plan below treats Credit-Builder the way streetwear treats a new silhouette — waitlist, drop day, creator hype cycle — because scarcity and momentum are what cut through in a feed where everyone claims to fix your finances.

---

## Tactic 1: The Waitlist Drop

We open a waitlist 30 days before launch with a live counter and a "first 10,000 get founding-member pricing locked forever" mechanic. Drop-day mechanics are the most reliable urgency play running right now, and a public counter turns sign-ups into content. Waitlist members get a numbered spot they can share as a card — screenshot bait, in a good way.

## Tactic 2: Creator Hype Cycle — "Watch My Score"

We recruit a wave of creators to document their scores climbing month over month, episode-style. Score-reveal content is huge right now — the numbers-going-up format is basically its own genre — and a serialized "Watch My Score" arc keeps creators (and their audiences) attached to the product for the whole flight instead of one sponsored post.

## Tactic 3: Podcast & Audio Blitz

Money podcasts and culture shows with younger audiences get host-read spots built around one line: "your rent already proves you pay on time — now make it count." Audio adds a layer of frequency the feed can't, and host-reads carry borrowed trust.

## Tactic 4: Drop-Day Stunt — The Score Board

On launch day we run a digital "Score Board" — a live, anonymized ticker of points gained across all Credit-Builder users — pushed across social, the app, and a Times-Square-style takeover moment we clip for content. Watching the community's collective score climb is the flywheel visual for the whole campaign.

## Tactic 5: Paid Short-Form Flight

A sustained paid flight on TikTok and Reels carries the glow-up creative: before/after score reveals, waitlist countdowns, creator clips, drop-day recaps. Paid keeps the hype cycle from being algorithm-dependent and retargets waitlist members who haven't converted.

---

## Target Audience

The audience for all of this is young adults with thin credit files — people in their twenties who pay rent on time, hold a job, maybe have never had a credit card, and keep getting dinged for having "insufficient history." They're on TikTok and Reels daily, they distrust banks but want the score, and they're already adjacent to Stackwise's saver base. The glow-up framing meets them as people leveling up, not people being rescued.

---

## Why This Works

Credit is the most emotionally loaded number in a young adult's life, and nobody has launched a credit product with actual drop-culture energy. The waitlist builds pressure, the drop releases it, the creator series sustains it, and the Score Board gives the whole thing a scoreboard. Stackwise gets a launch that feels like an event, not an announcement.
