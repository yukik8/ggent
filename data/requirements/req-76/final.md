## Opening: The Problem We Are Actually Solving

Stackwise's users have a locked door in front of them: they pay rent and bills on time, but thin credit files keep pricing them out of apartments, cars, and cards. The audience already trusts apps in this category — 58% of US adults under 35 used a budgeting or savings app in 2025, up from 44% in 2022 (ref-13) — but a credit product asks for a deeper commitment than a savings bucket, from a cohort with a hair-trigger for anything that smells like a finance scheme: 61% of savers under 30 say they distrust finance apps that lead with investment returns instead of safety (ref-13).

Credit-Builder is our answer to that locked-door problem: a $5/month add-on that reports on-time payments to the bureaus, with no credit check, no interest, and no card. The launch mechanics below — waitlist, drop day, creator series — are engineered to turn that answer into an event, and every tactic maps back to the named problem: making a thin file thicker, visibly, month after month.

## Tactics

### Tactic 1: The Waitlist Drop
Because the core problem is inertia — thin-file users assume credit products are not for them — the waitlist converts passive interest into a committed queue before launch. The founding-member pricing lock gives the drop urgency without discount-led messaging, which this audience largely ignores: only 22% respond to discount-led messaging as a first touch, while 49% respond to problem-solution storytelling (ref-07). The story is the locked door; the waitlist is the line forming in front of it.

### Tactic 2: Creator Hype Cycle — "Watch My Score"
Because the product's promise is a number that rises, the proof must be numbers rising on camera. 58% of Gen-Z respondents say they distrust ads that make claims without visible evidence or numbers (ref-07), and the serialized score-reveal format supplies that evidence monthly. We deliberately cast niche creators over celebrities: 64% of Gen-Z respondents have purchased a product recommended by a niche creator with under 100K followers (ref-07), and creator-partnership posts deliver 31% lower cost-per-engagement than brand-owned posts (ref-06).

### Tactic 3: Podcast & Audio Blitz
Host-read spots on money and culture podcasts add a trust-borrowing layer of frequency around one line: "your rent already proves you pay on time — now make it count." This matters for a considered sign-up, because Gen-Z respondents check an average of 2.7 sources before a first purchase over $50 (ref-07); audio makes Stackwise one of those sources.

### Tactic 4: Drop-Day Stunt — The Score Board
The live, anonymized ticker of points gained across all Credit-Builder users turns the community's collective progress into the campaign's flagship visual — evidence at scale, in a category where evidence is the currency of belief.

### Tactic 5: Paid Short-Form Flight
Because the audience discovers products in the feed — 71% of Gen-Z respondents discover new brands primarily through short-form video (ref-07) — the paid flight carries the glow-up creative on TikTok and Reels, where short-form video app-install campaigns average a $4.10 CPI for finance apps, versus $6.80 on search (ref-13). Frequency stays capped, since frequency above 4 exposures per week correlates with an 18% drop in engagement rate (ref-06).

## Target Audience

Young adults with thin credit files: twenty-somethings who pay rent on time, hold jobs, and keep getting refused for "insufficient history." They are adjacent to Stackwise's saver base and fluent in short-form video. The glow-up framing addresses them as people leveling up, not people being rescued — with the safety-first, no-yield positioning this cohort demands (ref-13).

## Why This Works

Credit is the most emotionally loaded number in a young adult's life, and this launch gives the locked-door problem a visible, serialized answer. The waitlist builds pressure, the drop releases it, the creator series proves the product monthly, and the Score Board makes collective progress the campaign's hero image — every element pointed at the same named problem and priced against benchmarked channel economics.
