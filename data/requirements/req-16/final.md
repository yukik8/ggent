## Campaign Title

**"Rep Your Region" — the Hometown Batch Flavor Showdown**

---

## Opening: The Problem We Are Actually Solving

Sunbrook's problem is not awareness of a new product; it is that young Midwest shoppers have no durable reason to choose Sunbrook over the store brand beside it. Private-label share of salty snacks rose from 18% to 23% between 2021 and 2024 (ref-01), and only 27% of snack buyers aged 18-34 can name a preferred mid-market crisp brand unprompted (ref-01). The damage compounds on the second purchase: repeat purchase rate for the average mid-market snack brand is 31%, versus 52% for the top-3 national brands (ref-01). Sunbrook does not need another flavor; it needs a flavor private label cannot copy and a mechanic that brings the same buyer back.

Hometown Batch is our answer to that specific problem. Five limited-edition regional flavors — Cleveland Pierogi & Caramelized Onion, Cincinnati-Style Chili, Chicago Italian Beef & Giardiniera, Detroit Coney, and Twin Cities Hotdish — each carrying its city's name, a retro postcard design, and a scan-to-vote code, at $4.29 per limited-edition bag. Regional identity is the one attribute a national private label cannot imitate, and the Showdown voting mechanic converts city pride into repeat purchases, not one-off trial. The category can support the premium play: the US salty snacks retail market was valued at $34.2B in 2024, growing 3.1% YoY (ref-01). Every tactic below is selected for its ability to close the repeat-purchase gap, not just generate buzz.

---

## Target Audience

Primary: snack buyers aged 18-34 in Midwest metros with strong regional identity — the exact segment where the 27% recall figure and the 31%-versus-52% repeat gap are measured (ref-01). They discover food brands where the Showdown will live: 71% of Gen-Z respondents discover new brands primarily through short-form video (ref-07). They are also allergic to empty claims — 58% say they distrust ads that make claims without visible evidence or numbers (ref-07) — and they respond to stories over coupons: only 22% respond to discount-led messaging as a first touch, while 49% respond to problem-solution storytelling (ref-07). The rivalry narrative, not a price promotion, is therefore the front door.

Secondary: displaced Midwesterners buying hometown nostalgia, and holiday shoppers picking up conversation-piece snacks for gatherings.

---

## The Big Idea

**"Rep Your Region"** turns a flavor launch into a friendly civic rivalry. Five cities, five flavors, one winner. Every bag purchased and scanned is a vote for your hometown, and in December we crown the winning city and give its flavor a promoted spot in the 2027 lineup. Because the core problem is repeat purchase, the mechanic matters strategically, not just creatively: voting rewards buying your city's bag again and again through the fall, directly attacking the gap where mid-market brands trail national brands 31% to 52% (ref-01). Matchup content, leaderboard updates, and city trash-talk templates keep the rivalry fed for fifteen weeks.

## Channel & Media Plan

Each channel below maps directly to the private-label-substitution and repeat-purchase problem named above.

### 1. Paid Social — Short-Form Video (Instagram Reels & TikTok)
Because young buyers cannot yet name Sunbrook unprompted, we spend where their brand discovery actually happens: 71% of this cohort discovers new brands primarily through short-form video (ref-07). The rivalry format also earns attention efficiently — short-form video ads average 2.4x the completion rate of static feed ads (ref-06), on inventory priced at a $9.40 average CPM for Instagram Reels and $7.80 for TikTok (ref-06). We will cap exposure deliberately, since frequency above 4 exposures per week correlates with an 18% drop in engagement rate (ref-06).

### 2. Local Micro-Creator Partnerships
Hometown credibility cannot come from a brand account, so each metro's flavor gets local "campaign captains." The trust economics are measured: 64% of Gen-Z respondents have purchased a product recommended by a niche creator with under 100K followers (ref-07), and creator-partnership posts deliver 31% lower cost-per-engagement than brand-owned posts (ref-06). Captain kits and a creator discount-code leaderboard extend the rivalry to the creators themselves.

### 3. In-Store Sampling — "Flavor Showdown" Weekends
Regional pride has to convert at shelf, and tasting is the strongest lever we have there: in-store sampling lifts trial conversion by 19% on average in grocery channels (ref-01). Voting-booth-styled stations where shoppers taste two rival flavors and cast a ballot turn that first taste into the entry point of the scan-to-vote repeat loop, with city-pride stickers as the take-home social artifact.

### 4. Grocery Retail Media
The private-label substitution happens at the digital shelf, so we defend it there with geo-matched placements that show each metro its own city's flavor first. Grocery retail media campaigns average ROAS of 3.8x for snack and beverage brands (ref-08), and on-site sponsored product placements drive 15% average lift in units per store per week (ref-08). At an average grocery retail media CPM of $12.60 versus $9.40 for Instagram Reels (ref-08), this channel is deployed surgically for conversion, not reach.

### 5. Email & SMS — the Showdown Ticker
This is the repeat-purchase engine. Every scanned bag opts the buyer into standings emails and lead-change alerts, and the behavioral benchmarks justify the investment: lifecycle-triggered emails average a 38% open rate, versus 21% for standard campaign sends (ref-16), and SMS opt-in subscribers convert at 2.4x the rate of email-only subscribers on time-limited offers (ref-16) — exactly the mechanics of a final-week voting push. Median ROI for retention marketing is $4.60 per dollar spent, versus $2.20 for prospecting (ref-16), which is why the ticker earns a dedicated line despite its modest budget share.

## Closing

This plan does not bet on a trend; it attacks the measured cause of Sunbrook's squeeze — a recall gap against national brands and a substitution threat from private label — with the one asset private label cannot copy: hometown identity. The rivalry builds the name, sampling converts it at shelf, retail media defends the digital shelf, and the Showdown ticker turns first bags into second and third bags, each tactic answerable to the same named problem.
