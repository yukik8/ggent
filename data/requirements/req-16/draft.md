## Campaign Title

**"Rep Your Region" — the Hometown Batch Flavor Showdown**

---

## Opening: Five Cities, Five Flavors, One Bag at a Time

Introducing Sunbrook Crisps Hometown Batch, a limited-edition series of five regional flavors inspired by iconic Midwest food traditions: Cleveland Pierogi & Caramelized Onion, Cincinnati-Style Chili, Chicago Italian Beef & Giardiniera, Detroit Coney, and Twin Cities Hotdish. Each bag carries its city's name front and center, a retro postcard-style design, and a scannable code that logs a "vote" for that city in the Showdown. At $4.29 per limited-edition bag, Hometown Batch sits proudly above the core line and feels like a collectible, not just a snack.

The timing could not be better. Local pride content is absolutely everywhere right now — city-nostalgia accounts, "only people from Ohio understand" memes, hometown food debates that rack up thousands of comments. Regional identity is one of the biggest trends in food culture at the moment, and limited drops are huge across every category from sneakers to seltzer. Hometown Batch lets Sunbrook ride both waves at once, and the voting mechanic adds a gamified layer on top, which is another thing consumers are really responding to lately.

---

## The Big Idea

**"Rep Your Region"** turns a flavor launch into a friendly civic rivalry. Five cities, five flavors, one winner. Every bag purchased and scanned is a vote for your hometown, every share is a flex, and in December we crown the winning city and give its flavor a promoted spot in the 2027 lineup. The bracket-style rivalry format is naturally shareable, it taps into the debate-bait content that performs so well on social, and it gives people a reason to buy their city's bag more than once. We can also spin up head-to-head matchup content, city trash-talk templates, leaderboard updates, reaction stitches — the content possibilities basically never run out.

---

## Channel & Media Plan

### 1. Paid Social — Short-Form Video (Instagram Reels & TikTok)
Short-form video is where food trends live and die, and hometown content performs incredibly well there. We will run matchup-style videos (Cleveland vs. Cincinnati week!), flavor reveal content, and leaderboard updates as paid Reels and TikTok flights. Debate-format content gets great engagement, so we will lean hard into the rivalry angle.

### 2. Local Micro-Creator Partnerships
We will recruit hometown creators in each of the five metros — local food reviewers, city-culture meme accounts, neighborhood personalities — to champion their city's flavor. Micro-creators are having a real moment and audiences trust them way more than big influencers. Each creator gets a "campaign captain" kit and a discount code leaderboard so the rivalry extends to the creators themselves.

### 3. In-Store Sampling — "Flavor Showdown" Weekends
Tasting stations at high-traffic grocery stores in each metro, styled as voting booths where shoppers taste two rival flavors and cast a paper ballot on the spot. Sampling always drives strong trial, and the booth format makes for great organic photo content too. Teams will hand out city-pride stickers, which people love to put on water bottles and laptops.

### 4. Grocery Retail Media
Sponsored placements and banners on our grocery partners' retail media networks, geo-matched so each metro sees its own city's flavor first. Retail media is one of the fastest-growing channels in advertising right now, and it keeps the Showdown visible right at the digital shelf where the purchase actually happens.

### 5. Email & SMS — the Showdown Ticker
Everyone who scans a bag joins the Showdown ticker: weekly standings emails, "your city just took the lead" SMS alerts, and a final-week push before voting closes. Lifecycle messaging is a super underrated channel and it gives us a direct line to the most engaged fans for the 2027 winner reveal.

---

## Target Audience

Primary: snack buyers aged 18-34 in Midwest metros with strong regional identity — people who put their area code in their bio and defend their city's food traditions in the comments. They snack often, they love limited drops, and social media is central to how they discover and talk about food. Secondary: displaced Midwesterners who buy hometown products out of nostalgia, and holiday shoppers grabbing conversation-piece snacks for gatherings.
