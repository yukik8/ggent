## Campaign Title

---

## Opening: The Problem We Are Actually Solving

Hartline's most valuable customers are its least secured. Independent contractors buy from Hartline weekly or daily, but nothing binds them: the moment a big-box pro desk offers a volume discount or a closer parking spot, the relationship evaporates, because there is no relationship — only a habit. The economics of fixing this are well documented: loyalty members generate 42% of revenue at brands with mature programs while representing 18% of customers (ref-16), and the median ROI for retention marketing is $4.60 per dollar spent, versus $2.20 for prospecting (ref-16). Hartline is currently spending nothing to retain the segment that a mature program would make its revenue backbone.

Pro Crew is our answer to that specific exposure: a 5% quarterly rebate with no points math, a dedicated pro desk with phone-ahead staging at all 40 stores, early-hours access, bulk pricing, and crew accounts. Because the core problem is an unsecured high-frequency segment, every feature is a switching cost in the contractor's favor — and every tactic below is built to enroll crews fast and make the first rebate check land.

| Program Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| 5% pro rebate tier | Quarterly payout on qualifying purchases |
| Dedicated pro desk | Priority service and phone-ahead order staging at all 40 stores |
| Early hours access | Doors open for members 30 minutes before public hours |
| Bulk pricing | Job-lot quantities on fasteners, lumber, and consumables |
| Crew accounts | One rebate account, multiple authorized buyers per crew |

---

## Target Audience

Independent contractors and small crews — remodelers, handymen, painters, landscapers, and specialty trades — in Hartline's lower-Midwest trade areas. High frequency, high annual value, zero contractual loyalty: exactly the profile the 42%-of-revenue loyalty economics describe (ref-16).

## The Big Idea

**"Your crew's got a crew."** Because the named problem is that Hartline's best customers have no reason to stay, Pro Crew positions Hartline as the supplier that treats a two-person operation like a valued account. The 5% rebate is the headline; the working-day benefits are the retention engine.

## Channel Plan

Each channel maps back to the unsecured-segment problem: enroll, activate, retain.

| Channel | Role |
|---|---|
| Field team & counter activation | Pro desk associates and a traveling field team sign crews up in person |
| Trade media (radio, jobsite OOH) | Drive-time radio and boards near supply routes and jobsite corridors |
| Email & SMS lifecycle program | Onboarding sequence, rebate statements, restock reminders |
| Paid search & social | Capture contractors searching for suppliers; retarget site visitors |
| Launch materials | Pro desk kits, crew welcome packs, vehicle decals |

### 1. Field Team & Counter Activation
Because the core problem is that the relationship lives only at the counter, that is where we formalize it. The pro desk moment also compounds: regional hardware chains retain 71% of customers who use in-store pickup, versus 48% of delivery-only customers (ref-14) — phone-ahead staging deliberately routes members through the store, not around it.

### 2. Trade Media
Drive-time radio and jobsite-corridor boards reach contractors during the working day. The message is the rebate and the desk, not a discount event — a retention promise, not a one-time price.

### 3. Email & SMS
Rebate statements and restock reminders are lifecycle moments, and the channel data favors them: lifecycle-triggered emails average a 38% open rate, versus 21% for standard campaign sends (ref-16). For time-sensitive early-hours events and bulk deals we lean on SMS, where SMS opt-in subscribers convert at 2.4x the rate of email-only subscribers on time-limited offers (ref-16).

### 4. Paid Search & Social
Search captures contractors actively comparing suppliers and rebate programs; social retargeting closes enrollment for site visitors who stalled at the form.
