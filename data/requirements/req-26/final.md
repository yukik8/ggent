## Campaign Title

**"Own the Cold" — the Northtrail Winter-Ready Push**

---

## Opening: The Problem We Are Actually Solving

Northtrail has a winter dormancy problem, not a product problem. Every November, a large share of Commuter One owners in snow-belt cities garage their bikes for four months. A parked bike breaks the daily habit the purchase was built on, invites the household back into the second car, and leaves the owner relationship silent for a third of the year — exactly when the service business should be earning its keep. The economics say this is the highest-leverage audience Northtrail can spend against: median ROI for retention marketing is $4.60 per dollar spent, versus $2.20 for prospecting (ref-16), and loyalty members generate 42% of revenue at brands with mature programs while representing 18% of customers (ref-16). Meanwhile the market itself validates the recurring-relationship model this campaign deepens: subscription and financing models grew to 21% of e-bike transactions in 2024, from 9% in 2022 (ref-03).

The $249 Winter-Riding Kit — all-weather tires, full-coverage fenders, a cold-weather battery jacket, bar mitts, and a high-output light set — and the $99/year Service Plan (pre-winter tune-up, spring refresh, priority battery-swap access, unlimited brake and drivetrain checks) are our answer to that specific problem. They directly remove the two stated reasons owners park the bike: cold-weather battery anxiety and maintenance uncertainty. The riding case survives winter untouched — the average commute distance in mid-size US cities is 7.8 miles, within a single e-bike battery charge (ref-03), snow or not. Every tactic below is selected for its ability to convert dormant winter owners into year-round riders and service-plan members, not to chase new buyers.

## Target Audience

Existing Commuter One owners in snow-belt cities — Madison, Minneapolis, Buffalo, Denver, Salt Lake City — where the dormancy problem is concentrated and measurable. These riders bought the bike as a car alternative, and their cities increasingly reward that choice: cities with protected bike lanes show 2.3x higher e-bike adoption than comparable cities without (ref-03), and every priority market on our list has expanded its protected network since these owners purchased. They are practical, review-reading, forum-dwelling riders who trust other owners over ads, and their single biggest winter objection — the battery in the cold — is precisely what the kit's battery jacket and the plan's priority swap access eliminate.

Because the core problem is a lapsed relationship rather than low awareness, we treat this as a retention audience first and a media audience second.

---

## The Big Idea

**"Own the Cold"** flips winter from the enemy into the badge. The centerpiece is the **First Frost Club**, a community program where owners who winterize their bike and log rides through the season earn milestone badges, from First Frost to Coldest Ride to Full Winter Rider. Because the core problem is dormancy, the club mechanic matters strategically, not just creatively: each logged ride is a habit maintained, each badge a reason to stay on the bike in the exact months the second car tries to win the commute back. The kit and the service plan are simply the equipment that gets you into the club.

## Channel & Media Plan

Each channel below maps directly to the dormancy problem named above.

### 1. Owner CRM — Email & SMS Lifecycle Program
Because the audience is entirely known owners, the CRM program is the campaign's spine, not a support channel. Lifecycle-triggered emails average a 38% open rate, versus 21% for standard campaign sends (ref-16), so the sequence triggers off each owner's city, first-frost date, and purchase anniversary rather than a broadcast calendar. SMS carries the time-boxed moments — event slots and pre-winter tune-up windows — because SMS opt-in subscribers convert at 2.4x the rate of email-only subscribers on time-limited offers (ref-16). Owners who parked the bike last winter get a dedicated win-back track, justified by the benchmark that cart-abandonment and lapsed-buyer flows recover 5.9% of at-risk revenue on average (ref-16).

### 2. Paid Social — Short-Form Video (Instagram Reels & YouTube)
Paid social exists here to make winter riding look normal and aspirational to people who already own the bike. Short-form video ads average 2.4x the completion rate of static feed ads (ref-06), which suits the atmospheric snow-riding creative, and Instagram Reels inventory is efficient at a $9.40 average CPM (ref-06). Targeting stays tight on owner lists and snow-belt lookalikes, and we cap delivery deliberately, since frequency above 4 exposures per week correlates with a 18% drop in engagement rate (ref-06) — an owner audience saturates fast.

### 3. Creator Partnerships — "A Full Winter, Documented"
This audience trusts riders over brands, so the proof burden falls on creators who actually live the winter. Snow-belt commuter creators will document an entire season on a winterized Commuter One — cold-morning starts, honest battery reports, weekly updates. The format is also the efficient choice: creator-partnership posts deliver 31% lower cost-per-engagement than brand-owned posts (ref-06), and the weekly episodes feed the paid social flight with evidence, not adjectives.

### 4. Winter-Ready Weekends — In-Person Install Events
Dormancy ends the day the kit is on the bike, so the events exist to compress the gap between intent and installation to zero. Owners ride in, get the kit installed and the service plan's pre-winter tune-up done, and roll out winter-ready the same day with a First Frost Club sticker. Each event doubles as service-plan enrollment on the spot, converting a $249 kit purchase into a $99/year recurring relationship while the owner is standing next to their freshly winterized bike.
