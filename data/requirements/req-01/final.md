## Campaign Title

**"Turn Up the Heat" — the Bold Batch Launch**

---

## Opening: The Problem We Are Actually Solving

Sunbrook has a recognition problem, not a product problem. Only 27% of snack buyers aged 18-34 can name a preferred mid-market crisp brand unprompted (ref-01), and private-label share of salty snacks rose from 18% to 23% between 2021 and 2024 (ref-01) — squeezing exactly the mid-market shelf position Sunbrook occupies. The consequence shows up in loyalty: repeat purchase rate for the average mid-market snack brand is 31%, versus 52% for the top-3 national brands (ref-01). Young Midwest shoppers are not rejecting Sunbrook; they simply cannot recall it when private label is fifty cents cheaper.

Bold Batch is our answer to that specific problem. A three-tier spicy line — Ember, Blaze, and Inferno, built on real chili blends with heat-level color-coded matte-black packaging — gives 18-34 buyers a reason to remember the Sunbrook name, at $3.99 per bag (a $0.50 premium over the core line) that reinforces distinctiveness from private label rather than competing with it on price. The category can support the move: US salty snacks retail was valued at $34.2B in 2024, growing 3.1% YoY (ref-01). Every tactic below is selected for its ability to move unaided recall and repeat purchase, not just trial.

---

## Target Audience

Primary: snack buyers aged 18-34 in Midwest grocery drive markets — the segment where the recall gap is measured and where private-label substitution hits hardest. This audience discovers brands through short-form video: 71% of Gen-Z respondents discover new brands primarily through short-form video (ref-07). They are also evidence-sensitive and story-driven — 58% say they distrust ads that make claims without visible evidence or numbers, and only 22% respond to discount-led messaging as a first touch while 49% respond to problem-solution storytelling (ref-07). Our creative therefore leads with the challenge story, never with a coupon.

Secondary: existing Sunbrook loyalists trading up, and household shoppers buying for gatherings.

---

## The Big Idea

**"Turn Up the Heat"** is a challenge-driven launch platform. We invite consumers to climb the three heat levels — starting at Ember, surviving Blaze, and daring to attempt Inferno — and to film and share their reactions. Because the core problem is unaided recall, the escalating-heat mechanic matters strategically, not just creatively: it forces repeat engagement with the Sunbrook name across three purchase occasions, converting a one-time trial into the repeat behavior where mid-market brands trail national brands 31% to 52% (ref-01). How far can you go?

## Channel & Media Plan

Each channel below maps directly to the recall-and-repeat problem named above.

### 1. Paid Social — Short-Form Video (Instagram Reels & TikTok)
Because the core problem is that young buyers cannot name Sunbrook unprompted, we put the heaviest weight where brand discovery actually happens: 71% of this cohort discovers new brands primarily through short-form video (ref-07). The format is also efficient and attention-holding — Instagram Reels average a $9.40 CPM and TikTok $7.80 (ref-06), and short-form video ads average 2.4x the completion rate of static feed ads (ref-06). We will cap frequency deliberately, since frequency above 4 exposures per week correlates with an 18% drop in engagement rate (ref-06).

### 2. Creator Partnerships
Creator content is how we make the challenge credible rather than advertised. 64% of Gen-Z respondents have purchased a product recommended by a niche creator with under 100K followers (ref-07), so we prioritize a roster of niche food and lifestyle creators over pure reach plays. The economics support it: creator-partnership posts deliver 31% lower cost-per-engagement than brand-owned posts (ref-06), and creator cut-downs feed the paid social flight.

### 3. In-Store Sampling & Shopper Activation
Recall means nothing if it never converts at shelf. In-store sampling lifts trial conversion by 19% on average in grocery channels (ref-01), so weekend "heat station" events at high-traffic Midwest grocery locations — sampling Ember and Blaze, with Inferno as the take-home dare — turn the social challenge into a physical first purchase, the entry point to the repeat loop.

### 4. Grocery Retail Media
Because the private-label squeeze happens at the digital point of purchase, we defend it there. Grocery retail media campaigns average a ROAS of 3.8x for snack and beverage brands (ref-08), and on-site sponsored product placements drive a 15% average lift in units per store per week (ref-08). At a $12.60 average grocery retail media CPM versus $9.40 for Reels (ref-08), we use this channel surgically for conversion, not reach.

## Why Sunbrook Wins

This plan does not just launch a spicy line; it attacks the measured cause of Sunbrook's squeeze — a recall gap against national brands and a price gap against private label — with a challenge platform engineered for memorability and repeat purchase. Social buzz builds the name, sampling converts it at shelf, and retail media defends the digital shelf, each tactic answerable to the same named problem.
