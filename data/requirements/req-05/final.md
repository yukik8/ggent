## Campaign Title

**"The Golden Hours: 48 Hours in Cascade Valley"**

---

## The Problem We Are Solving

Cascade Valley has an empty-rooms problem, not a beauty problem. Shoulder-season (Apr-May, Sep-Oct) occupancy for regional destinations averages 46%, versus 78% peak-season (ref-05). The Valley's inns, restaurants, and outfitters are staffed and open through September and October while nearly half their capacity sits unsold, and at an average regional weekend visitor spend of $412 per trip across lodging, food, and activities (ref-05), every unfilled fall weekend is real revenue left on the table.

The Golden Hours is our answer to that specific problem: a campaign engineered to convert drive-market travelers under 35 into booked shoulder-season weekends, in the exact window when the Valley is most cinematic and least occupied. Every tactic below is selected for its ability to fill September and October rooms, not just to generate applause.

## Target Audience

Drive-market travelers under 35 within roughly 4 hours of Cascade Valley. This is a deliberately tight focus, because it matches how regional trips actually get booked: drive-market weekend trips make up 61% of visits to small US regional destinations (ref-05), and 54% of travelers under 35 pick regional trips based on short-form video seen in the prior 30 days (ref-05). That 30-day decision window maps precisely onto our flight plan: content seen in September converts into October weekends. Couples and young families inside the drive market remain a secondary audience, but media and creative are optimized against the under-35 booker.

## The Big Idea

**Golden hour only happens twice a day, and the Valley's golden season only happens once a year.** The Golden Hours frames the shoulder season as the Valley's most cinematic window, and packages it as a ready-made 48-hour itinerary. We will produce a series of "48 Hours in Cascade Valley" itinerary films, each following one real weekend from Friday arrival to Sunday send-off: sunrise on the ridge trail, lunch at the farm market, an afternoon paddle, dinner on a lit-up main street. Every film ends with the same simple promise: your golden weekend is one booking away.

Because the core problem is unsold shoulder-season capacity, the creative is built as problem-solution storytelling rather than destination bragging. Only 22% of Gen-Z respondents respond to discount-led messaging as a first touch, while 49% respond to problem-solution storytelling (ref-07), so the films answer the audience's real question, "what would we actually do there for a weekend," instead of leading with the $400 package price.

## Channel & Media Plan

Each channel below is the junior team's original selection. What follows is why each one earns its place against the occupancy problem.

1. **Short-form video (Instagram Reels, TikTok, YouTube Shorts)** — the campaign's engine, because this is where the booking decision starts: 71% of Gen-Z respondents discover new brands primarily through short-form video (ref-07). The format also works harder per dollar, with short-form video ads averaging 2.4x the completion rate of static feed ads (ref-06), on efficient inventory at a $9.40 average CPM for Reels, $7.80 for TikTok, and $6.90 for Shorts (ref-06). We will cap delivery below four exposures per user per week, since frequency above 4 exposures per week correlates with a 18% drop in engagement rate (ref-06).
2. **Creator partnerships** — regional travel and outdoors creators produce their own 48-hour weekends in the Valley. This is a conversion play, not a reach play: 64% of Gen-Z respondents have purchased a product recommended by a niche creator with under 100K followers (ref-07), and creator-partnership posts deliver 31% lower cost-per-engagement than brand-owned posts (ref-06), so we prioritize niche drive-market creators over large national names.
3. **Content production** — a two-wave shoot (early fall and peak foliage) producing the hero itinerary films plus a library of cutdowns. The cutdown library also seeds an always-on posting cadence for the Board beyond the campaign window, because destinations running always-on social content see 27% higher repeat visitation than campaign-only destinations (ref-05). This campaign should be the start of a system, not a one-off burst.
4. **Booking hub and email** — a dedicated "Golden Hours" landing page with the 48-hour itineraries, package booking at the $400-per-visitor positioning, and an email capture flow. The email layer exists because under-35 travelers verify before they buy, checking an average of 2.7 sources before a first purchase over $50 (ref-07). Email nurture keeps us present across that verification loop until the booking lands.
5. **Metro digital out-of-home** — digital boards and transit screens in the two largest drive-market metros, timed to Thursday and Friday commutes. With drive-market trips at 61% of regional visitation (ref-05), OOH in the origin metros is the physical reminder layered on top of the digital decision journey.

## KPIs

Every KPI ladders back to the occupancy problem.

- **Occupancy (primary):** lift September–October lodging occupancy from the regional 46% shoulder-season baseline toward the 78% peak-season benchmark (ref-05)
- **Bookings:** package bookings attributable to the campaign, valued against the $412 average visitor spend per trip (ref-05)
- **Media efficiency:** paid social click-through at or above the 1.2% cross-vertical average, targeting the 2.6% top-quartile benchmark (ref-06)
- **Engagement:** creator content cost-per-engagement tracking to the 31% advantage over brand-owned posts (ref-06)
- **Pipeline:** email captures from the booking hub as the nurture pool for the 2.7-source verification journey (ref-07)
