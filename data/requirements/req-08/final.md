# Campaign Proposal: "Swap & Go" — Northtrail Battery-Swap Membership Push

**Prepared by:** Ryo Tanabe, Junior Planner — revised by Sofia Reyes, Creative Strategy Director, Meridian & Craft
**Client:** Northtrail Mobility
**Product:** Battery-swap subscription (standalone membership, $39/month)
**Budget:** $350,000
**Flight:** February 1 – May 31, 2026

---

## 1. The Problem We Are Solving

Northtrail has a price-barrier problem, not a product problem. 68% of urban commuters aged 25-40 say upfront price is the main barrier to buying an e-bike (ref-03) — and today, Northtrail's revenue is still concentrated in exactly the kind of upfront purchase those commuters are refusing to make. Meanwhile the market has already shown where demand is moving: subscription and financing models grew to 21% of e-bike transactions in 2024, from 9% in 2022 (ref-03). Northtrail is sitting on a $39/month product built for that shift, but neither existing owners nor price-blocked commuters know it exists as something they can buy on its own.

The battery-swap membership is our answer to that specific problem. At $39/month, it converts the two audiences the upfront price wall currently locks out or leaves under-monetized: owners who paid once and never spend again, and commuters who want in without a four-figure purchase. Every tactic below is selected for its ability to dismantle the price barrier and drive membership sign-ups, not just visibility.

## 2. Target Audience

Because the core problem is the price barrier, we split the audience by their relationship to it:

- **Existing Northtrail e-bike owners.** They cleared the price wall already; for them the membership is a recurring-revenue upgrade. Their average commute is well inside a single charge — 7.8 miles in mid-size US cities (ref-03) — so the pitch is convenience and never charging at home, not range.
- **Price-sensitive commuters aged 25-40.** These are the 68% blocked by upfront price (ref-03). The membership reframes Northtrail from a $1,900 decision into a $39 one, and we concentrate spend in cities with protected bike lanes, where e-bike adoption runs 2.3x higher than in comparable cities without (ref-03).

## 3. The Big Idea: "Swap & Go"

The creative platform is unchanged: **"Swap & Go — power that never runs out."** Every asset dramatizes the sixty-second swap — a rider pulls up, trades batteries, and rides off before their coffee goes cold. What changes is the framing: each spot opens on the problem (the dead battery, the $1,900 sticker, the apartment with no outlet near the bike) and resolves it with the swap. That structure matters because 49% of respondents respond to problem-solution storytelling as a first touch, versus only 22% for discount-led messaging (ref-07) — so the story leads, and the "first month free" offer closes.

We keep the draft's commitment to real riders and real stations over studio shots.

## 4. Channel & Media Plan

Same channels as drafted; each now carries its evidence and maps back to the price-barrier problem.

| Channel | Role & justification |
|---|---|
| **Short-form video (Reels, TikTok, YouTube Shorts)** | Hero channel. 71% of Gen-Z respondents discover new brands primarily through short-form video (ref-07), and the inventory is efficient: Instagram Reels average CPM is $9.40, TikTok $7.80, YouTube Shorts $6.90 (ref-06). Short-form video ads also average 2.4x the completion rate of static feed ads (ref-06) — essential when the whole story is a sixty-second swap. We cap frequency at four exposures per week, since frequency above 4 correlates with an 18% drop in engagement rate (ref-06). |
| **Creator partnerships** | Local cycling and commuter-lifestyle creators document a month of swap-only riding. Creator-partnership posts deliver 31% lower cost-per-engagement than brand-owned posts (ref-06), and 64% of this cohort have purchased a product recommended by a niche creator with under 100K followers (ref-07) — so we brief niche local creators, not national names. |
| **Out-of-home near bike infrastructure** | Posters and station wraps along protected bike lanes — the corridors where adoption is already 2.3x higher (ref-03) — catching riders at the exact moment of relevance. |
| **Owned CRM / email** | Direct offer to the existing owner base: first month free when they activate before March 31. This is the cheapest sign-up we will buy all flight, aimed squarely at converting one-time buyers into recurring revenue. |
| **Campaign landing page & content** | Station map, membership math ($39/month versus the upfront wall), rider testimonials. The math module exists because the barrier is price (ref-03); the page's job is to make $39 feel obvious. |

## 7. KPIs

Re-anchored to the problem: the campaign succeeds if it converts price-blocked commuters and idle owners into paying members.

- Membership sign-ups during the flight (primary), split by owner vs. new-rider cohort
- Conversion rate from the existing owner base off the CRM offer
- Paid-social click-through rate, benchmarked against the 1.2% cross-vertical average with top-quartile 2.6% as the stretch target (ref-06)
- Video completion rate vs. static benchmarks (ref-06)
- Cost per landing-page visit and per sign-up by channel
- Awareness lift of the swap membership among commuters 25-40 (survey)

## 8. Why This Will Work

The market is growing — US e-bike unit sales hit 1.4M in 2024, a 12% YoY increase (ref-03) — but Northtrail's constraint is not category demand, it is the upfront price wall that 68% of its target commuters name as their main barrier (ref-03). The industry's own transaction mix, with subscriptions climbing from 9% to 21% in two years (ref-03), tells us buyers want exactly the payment structure this membership offers. "Swap & Go" doesn't just launch a feature; it removes the single number that stands between Northtrail and its next cohort of riders, and it turns every past buyer into recurring revenue at $39 a month.
