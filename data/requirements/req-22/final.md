## Campaign Title

**"The Starting Lineup" — the Velura Foundations Men's Launch**

---

## Opening: The Problem We Are Actually Solving

Velura has an audience ceiling problem and a second-order problem, and the men's launch has to solve both at once. The brand's growth sits almost entirely on a female-skewing moisturizer franchise inside a category where the average DTC skincare brand loses 44% of first-time buyers before a second order (ref-02) — every new customer Velura acquires from the existing audience is fighting that same leak. Meanwhile the category headroom is real: the US clean-beauty segment reached $11.6B in 2024, up 8.4% YoY while total prestige skincare grew only 2.9% (ref-02), and men new to skincare are the largest untapped buyer pool Velura has never addressed.

Velura Foundations is our answer to that specific problem. A three-SKU numbered routine — gel cleanser, a lightweight version of the hero moisturizer, and a daily SPF fluid, priced $24-$38 across three SKUs — converts a first purchase into a habit by design, because a routine is a repeat-purchase engine, not a single jar. The moisturizer at its center is deliberate: moisturizer is the highest-frequency skincare repurchase category at 3.4 units per buyer per year (ref-02), which means every man who finishes step 02 comes back. Every tactic below is selected for its ability to open the new audience and close the second-order leak, not just to generate launch buzz.

---

## Target Audience

Primary: men aged 22-38 who are new to multi-step skincare — currently a bar of soap, maybe an aftershave balm, and whatever moisturizer a partner left in the bathroom. They are curious but intimidated, and they are reachable exactly where discovery happens for the younger half of this cohort: 71% of Gen-Z respondents discover new brands primarily through short-form video (ref-07). They are also evidence-sensitive — 58% say they distrust ads that make claims without visible evidence or numbers (ref-07), and only 22% respond to discount-led messaging as a first touch while 49% respond to problem-solution storytelling (ref-07). Our creative therefore leads with the "you don't know where to start" problem and the numbered answer, never with a promo code. Velura's clean, fragrance-free credentials do real work here too: 62% of skincare buyers under 30 say ingredient transparency is a top-3 purchase driver (ref-02).

Secondary: partners and family members buying a starter routine as a gift in the holiday window, and existing Velura customers introducing the brand to the men in their household.

---

## The Big Idea

**"The Starting Lineup"** frames the three SKUs like a roster: three players, three positions, one team. Cleanser is your opener, moisturizer is your anchor, SPF is your closer. Because the core problem is second-order churn, the roster mechanic matters strategically, not just creatively: a numbered 01-02-03 system gives a first-time buyer a defined behavior to complete and a defined reason to replenish, attacking the 44% first-order loss rate (ref-02) at the level of product habit rather than discounting. The invitation stays simple: you don't need to figure skincare out, you just need a starting lineup.

## Channel & Media Plan

Each channel below maps directly to the two named problems — opening the male audience and closing the second-order leak.

### 1. Paid Social — Short-Form Video (TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts)
Because the new audience has to be acquired where it discovers brands, we weight short-form video heaviest: 71% of Gen-Z respondents discover new brands primarily through short-form video (ref-07). The inventory is efficient across all three platforms — Instagram Reels average a $9.40 CPM, TikTok $7.80, and YouTube Shorts $6.90 (ref-06) — and the format holds attention, with short-form video ads averaging 2.4x the completion rate of static feed ads (ref-06). We will cap frequency deliberately, since frequency above 4 exposures per week correlates with a 18% drop in engagement rate (ref-06), and keep a rapid-response editing capability on retainer to jump on formats as they emerge.

### 2. Creator Partnerships — Grooming, Fitness & Everyday Guys
Because this audience distrusts polish, creators carry the credibility load. 64% of Gen-Z respondents have purchased a product recommended by a niche creator with under 100K followers (ref-07), so the roster prioritizes niche grooming, fitness, and everyday-guy creators documenting their first thirty days on the lineup over pure reach plays. The economics support the same choice: creator-partnership posts deliver 31% lower cost-per-engagement than brand-owned posts (ref-06), and creator cut-downs feed the paid flight.

### 3. Specialty Retail Activation
Because Velura's retail expansion and the men's launch must reinforce each other, Foundations spearheads the shelf. Specialty retail accounts for 38% of clean-beauty discovery, versus 24% for brand-owned sites (ref-02) — for a buyer who has never visited a skincare DTC site, the beauty-chain aisle is the front door. "Starting Lineup" end-caps with tester stations, a three-step counter card, and a one-page staff playbook turn that discovery share into first purchases, including the gift purchases that dominate the holiday phase.

### 4. Lifecycle Email & SMS — The 30-Day Routine Coach
This channel is aimed squarely at the 44% first-order leak (ref-02). Every Foundations buyer enrolls in a "Routine Coach" flow across their first month, and the mechanics favor us: lifecycle-triggered emails average a 38% open rate, versus 21% for standard campaign sends (ref-16), SMS opt-in subscribers convert at 2.4x the rate of email-only subscribers on time-limited offers (ref-16), and the median ROI for retention marketing is $4.60 per dollar spent, versus $2.20 for prospecting (ref-16). Once the habit is formed, the flow cross-sells the core Velura franchise.

## Why Velura Wins

This plan does not just launch a men's line; it attacks the two measured constraints on Velura's growth — an audience ceiling and a 44% first-order leak — with a routine engineered to be started and, more importantly, restarted. Short-form video and creators open the new audience where it actually discovers brands, specialty retail converts it at the shelf that drives 38% of clean-beauty discovery, and the Routine Coach turns the first purchase into the repeat behavior the category fails to secure, each tactic answerable to the same named problems.
