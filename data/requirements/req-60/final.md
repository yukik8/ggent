## Campaign Title

**"Stronger at Home" — the Kinetiq Strength Kit Launch**

---

## Opening: The Problem We Are Actually Solving

Kinetiq's growth constraint is not the size of the fitness market; it is that the company's cheapest revenue is sitting inside its own member base while most marketing dollars chase strangers. The benchmark math is stark: median ROI for retention marketing is $4.60 per dollar spent, versus $2.20 for prospecting (ref-16), and at brands with mature programs, loyalty members generate 42% of revenue while representing 18% of customers (ref-16). Kinetiq's members are exactly that concentrated-value audience — hardware owners, monthly subscribers, one click from an upsell — and until now Kinetiq has had nothing new to sell them.

The $495 Strength Kit is our answer to that constraint: adjustable dumbbells, an anchored band system, and a heart-rate arm band that unlock a new strength class category and turn every Row One into a full home gym.

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Price | $495 strength kit add-on |
| Contents | Adjustable dumbbells, anchored band system, HR arm band |
| Software | New strength class category, combined row+strength programs |
| Compatibility | All Row One units, no installation required |
| Availability | Member presale in March, public launch in April |

There is a second, quieter payoff: variety defends the habit that retention depends on. Members completing 8 or more workouts in their first month show 81% 12-month retention, versus 34% for those completing fewer (ref-11) — and a second modality gives every member more ways to keep the workout count climbing. Every tactic below is sequenced member-first because that is where the return is measured.

---

## Target Audience

Primary: existing Kinetiq members — the 18%-style high-value core that loyalty benchmarks show can drive 42% of revenue (ref-16). They already trust the brand, own the hardware, and can add the kit in one click. Secondary: strength-curious prospects who have hesitated on a rower-only purchase because they want a fuller workout from a single setup. The budget deliberately weights the primary audience's channels first.

---

## The Big Idea

**"Stronger at Home."** One corner of your home now covers your whole workout. Because the core problem is monetizing the owned audience before chasing a new one, the creative speaks first to the member: same member, same corner, same hour of the morning — rowing on Monday, pressing on Tuesday, both in one session on Thursday. Your rower just got a second act. For prospects, the same footage becomes the complete-home-gym story: everything, in two square feet.

## Channel & Media Plan

Each channel below maps directly to the member-first revenue problem named above.

| Channel | Role |
|---|---|
| Member lifecycle — email, push & in-app | Presale announcement, launch offers, upgrade journeys |
| Live strength launch class series | New class category debut with launch-week live events |
| Paid social — short-form video | Prospect-facing reach for the complete-home-gym story |
| Creator partnerships | Members and fitness creators showing real row+strength routines |

### 1. Member Lifecycle — Email, Push & In-App
Because the core problem is that the highest-ROI audience is the one Kinetiq already owns, the launch opens here: presale announcement, in-app banners, push, and upgrade journeys for members who click but do not buy. The channel mechanics justify the sequencing — lifecycle-triggered emails average a 38% open rate, versus 21% for standard campaign sends (ref-16), and the presale deadline gives SMS-style urgency levers real teeth, since SMS opt-in subscribers convert at 2.4x the rate of email-only subscribers on time-limited offers (ref-16).

### 2. Live Strength Launch Class Series
Launch week debuts the strength category with daily live classes and coach-led kit walkthroughs. Because retention runs through workout frequency (ref-11), the class series is not launch theater — it is how the kit converts from a purchase into added weekly workouts.

### 3. Paid Social — Short-Form Video
For prospects, short-form video carries the complete-home-gym story efficiently: Instagram Reels average a $9.40 CPM, TikTok $7.80, and YouTube Shorts $6.90 (ref-06), and short-form video ads average 2.4x the completion rate of static feed ads (ref-06) — the two-in-one demo needs the full 30 seconds to land. Delivery stays capped, since frequency above 4 exposures per week correlates with an 18% drop in engagement rate (ref-06).

### 4. Creator Partnerships
Fitness creators and real members show their actual weekly routines with the kit. Beyond credibility, the buy is efficient: creator-partnership posts deliver 31% lower cost-per-engagement than brand-owned posts (ref-06).

## Why Kinetiq Wins

This launch treats Kinetiq's member base as what the benchmarks say it is — the most profitable audience the brand will ever have (ref-16) — and sells it a second act before spending a dollar on strangers.
