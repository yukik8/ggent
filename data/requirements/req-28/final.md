## Campaign Title

## Opening: The Problem We Are Actually Solving

Northtrail's Denver problem is not awareness of e-bikes — it is the gap between wanting one and committing $1,899 to one. 68% of urban commuters aged 25-40 say upfront price is the main barrier to buying an e-bike (ref-03), and this is a considered purchase: Gen-Z respondents check an average of 2.7 sources before a first purchase over $50 (ref-07). A commuter who has never felt the ride, never seen a battery swap, and never done the cost math against their car payment will keep deferring the decision indefinitely. That deferral is the problem this launch attacks.

The market is moving in Northtrail's favor — US e-bike unit sales hit 1.4M in 2024, a 12% YoY increase (ref-03), and subscription and financing models grew to 21% of e-bike transactions in 2024, from 9% in 2022 (ref-03) — but growth flows to whichever brand collapses the barrier first. The Denver flagship is our answer to that specific problem: a two-floor RiNo space with a live battery-swap demo wall, an indoor test-ride loop, and supervised street test rides, where the Commuter One at $1,899 and the cargo model at $2,799 stop being sticker prices and become a felt experience with the math laid out. Denver is the right stage — cities with protected bike lanes show 2.3x higher e-bike adoption than comparable cities without (ref-03). Every tactic below is selected for one job: moving barrier-blocked commuters from "someday" to a completed test ride.

---

## The Big Idea

**"Trade Your Traffic"** invites Denverites to trade one rush-hour drive for one test ride. Because the core problem is a commitment barrier that only firsthand experience dissolves, the swap mechanic is strategic, not just playful: it reframes the decision from "spend $1,899" to "trade thirty bad minutes for thirty good ones," and it points every piece of media at the single behavior — the test ride — that converts consideration into purchase. This is also the story structure the audience actually responds to: 49% of Gen-Z respondents respond to problem-solution storytelling as a first touch, versus only 22% for discount-led messaging (ref-07). Billboards troll traffic where traffic is worst, creators film POV "traded my drive" videos, and the flagship is where the trade happens.

## Channel & Media Plan

Each channel below maps directly to the barrier-to-test-ride problem named above.

### 1. Short-Form Paid Social (TikTok & Instagram Reels)
Because the blocked commuter has to see the trade before making it, we put the heaviest media weight where discovery happens: 71% of Gen-Z respondents discover new brands primarily through short-form video (ref-07). The format is efficient and attention-holding — TikTok averages a $7.80 CPM and Instagram Reels $9.40 (ref-06), and short-form video ads average 2.4x the completion rate of static feed ads (ref-06). Test-ride POV footage, battery-swap demo clips, and showroom walkthroughs run in native-style edits, and we cap delivery deliberately, since frequency above 4 exposures per week correlates with a 18% drop in engagement rate (ref-06).

### 2. Denver Creator Partnerships
A skeptical, multi-source buyer needs proof from someone with their exact life, not a brand claim. 64% of Gen-Z respondents have purchased a product recommended by a niche creator with under 100K followers (ref-07), so our roster of Denver bike commuters and lifestyle creators skews niche and local, each filming their own "Trade Your Traffic" episode ending at the flagship. The economics support it: creator-partnership posts deliver 31% lower cost-per-engagement than brand-owned posts (ref-06), and creator footage feeds the paid flight.

### 3. Traffic-Trolling OOH & Transit
Because the target's problem is most vivid while they are sitting in it, boards along I-25 and Colfax ("You could be past this by now") and bus shelter and light-rail placements catch commuters at the exact moment the pain is real, and route them to the showroom while the frustration is fresh. Clever boards get screenshotted and reposted, extending the buy into earned social.

### 4. Pop-Up Test-Ride Tour
The ride is the conversion event, so we take it to where barrier-blocked commuters already are: a branded trailer with demo bikes at farmers markets, breweries, and trailhead lots all summer, funneling riders to the flagship. Every rider opts into follow-up, and that follow-up is engineered, not incidental — lifecycle-triggered emails average a 38% open rate, versus 21% for standard campaign sends (ref-16), and SMS opt-in subscribers convert at 2.4x the rate of email-only subscribers on time-limited offers (ref-16), so each test ride becomes a tracked path to purchase, including financing options as they roll out.

### 5. Geofenced Mobile & Paid Search
Geofenced mobile around downtown parking garages, park-and-rides, and competitor bike shops, plus paid search on Denver-metro e-bike and commuter terms, captures the demand the rest of the campaign creates — the bottom of the funnel for a buyer who checks 2.7 sources before committing (ref-07).

## Target Audience

Primary: Denver metro commuters aged 25-45 who are test-ride-curious but purchase-blocked. This is the 68% for whom upfront price is the main barrier (ref-03) — drivers who resent their commute costs, live within realistic riding distance of work (average commute distance in mid-size US cities is 7.8 miles, within a single e-bike battery charge, ref-03), and need to feel the ride and see the math before committing. They discover through short-form video, trust niche creators over ads, and respond to problem-solution stories rather than discounts (ref-07).

Secondary: weekend recreation riders, and families evaluating the $2,799 cargo model as a second-car replacement.

This plan does not just open a showroom; it attacks the measured barrier — upfront price fear that only a felt test ride and honest math can dissolve — with a swap mechanic that turns Denver's worst commutes into Northtrail's best invitations.
