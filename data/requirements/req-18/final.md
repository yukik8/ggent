## Campaign Title

**"Pack the Week" — the Sunbrook Variety-Pack Repositioning**

---

## Opening: The Problem We Are Actually Solving

Sunbrook has a frequency problem, not a product problem. The variety pack sells as an occasional party purchase in a category where the money is made weekly, and the shoppers who could buy it every week are defaulting to habit and to store brands: private-label share of salty snacks rose from 18% to 23% between 2021 and 2024 (ref-01), and repeat purchase rate for the average mid-market snack brand is 31%, versus 52% for the top-3 national brands (ref-01). That 21-point repeat gap is the entire economics of this SKU. The category itself is healthy — the US salty snacks retail market was valued at $34.2B in 2024, growing 3.1% YoY (ref-01) — so the opportunity is not to grow the pie but to move Sunbrook's pack from the party list to the weekly list.

The repositioned 8-count pack is our answer to that specific problem. Eight single-serve bags across four flavors — Original ×2, Sea Salt & Vinegar ×2, Honey BBQ ×2, and a rotating "Flavor of the Season" ×2 — at $7.99, in a carry-handle carton with a fold-down pantry dispenser and day-dot bags. The rotating slot exists precisely to break the store-brand substitution habit: the pack is never the same purchase twice, which is something private label cannot cheaply imitate. Every tactic below is selected for its ability to convert occasional buyers into weekly buyers, and each section names how it does so.

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Format | 8-count single-serve variety pack |
| Flavors | Original ×2, Sea Salt & Vinegar ×2, Honey BBQ ×2, rotating seasonal ×2 |
| Price | $7.99 per pack |
| Packaging | Carry-handle carton, fold-down dispenser panel, day-dot bags |
| Placement | Multipack aisle, Midwest grocery chains |

## Target Audience

Primary: household shoppers aged 30-49 who buy snack multipacks weekly — the segment where the repeat gap is measured and where the weekly-list habit either includes Sunbrook or funds private label. These shoppers plan trips, shop from lists, and increasingly build their baskets online before they ever reach the aisle, which is why the digital shelf carries real weight in this plan. They are also the audience most reachable through the retailer relationships they already trust: loyalty members generate 42% of revenue at brands with mature programs while representing 18% of customers (ref-16), and this plan deliberately routes retention spend through those programs.

Secondary: households that currently buy variety packs only for parties or holidays. Because the core problem is frequency, this group is not an afterthought — they are the conversion pool the whole campaign draws from.

---

## The Big Idea

**"Pack the Week"** repositions the Sunbrook variety pack from a party-occasion purchase to a standing item on the weekly grocery list. The creative frames the pack as the week's snack plan solved in one grab: eight bags, five school days, two weekend outings, one decision. Because the core problem is a 31%-versus-52% repeat gap (ref-01), the weekly-calendar visual device is doing strategic work, not decoration — it plants the pack inside the shopper's existing weekly planning ritual, which is where repeat purchase is actually decided. All assets — social video, retail media placements, in-store displays, and email — repeat that one device.

## Channel & Media Plan

Each channel below maps directly to the frequency problem named above.

| # | Channel | Role in plan |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Paid social (Facebook + Instagram Reels) | Awareness of the repositioned pack |
| 2 | Grocery retail media | Conversion at the digital shelf |
| 3 | In-store shopper marketing & sampling | Trial and visibility in aisle |
| 4 | CRM — email & SMS via retailer programs | Repeat purchase and habit building |

### 1. Paid Social — Facebook + Instagram Reels
Because occasional buyers will not reconsider a pack they think they already know, awareness of the repositioning has to be rebuilt, and short-form video does it efficiently: Instagram Reels average a $9.40 CPM (ref-06) and short-form video ads average 2.4x the completion rate of static feed ads (ref-06). The "Pack the Week" videos — the Sunday grocery haul, the pantry dispenser moment, kids picking their day-dot bags — dramatize the weekly ritual we are selling into. We will cap exposure deliberately, since frequency above 4 exposures per week correlates with an 18% drop in engagement rate (ref-06).

### 2. Grocery Retail Media
Because the weekly list is increasingly built online, the digital shelf is where the store-brand swap happens — and where we intercept it. Grocery retail media campaigns average a ROAS of 3.8x for snack and beverage brands (ref-08), and on-site sponsored product placements drive a 15% average lift in units per store per week (ref-08) — units per store per week being precisely the metric a frequency repositioning must move. At a $12.60 average grocery retail media CPM versus $9.40 for Instagram Reels (ref-08), we use this channel for conversion rather than reach, and we are moving with the market: 68% of CPG brands plan to increase retail media budgets in 2026 (ref-08).

### 3. In-Store Shopper Marketing & Sampling
A weekly habit still starts with a first taste. In-store sampling lifts trial conversion by 19% on average in grocery channels (ref-01), so weekend sampling of the rotating seasonal flavor — the pack's most defensible feature against private label — plus weekly-calendar end-caps in the multipack aisle convert the awareness built on social into the first purchase the CRM engine then works on.

### 4. CRM — Email & SMS Through Retailer Loyalty Programs
This is the channel that attacks the repeat gap directly, and the economics favor it decisively: median ROI for retention marketing is $4.60 per dollar spent, versus $2.20 for prospecting (ref-16). The lifecycle flows are built on measured behavior, not hope — lifecycle-triggered emails average a 38% open rate, versus 21% for standard campaign sends (ref-16), SMS opt-in subscribers convert at 2.4x the rate of email-only subscribers on time-limited offers (ref-16), and cart-abandonment and lapsed-buyer flows recover 5.9% of at-risk revenue on average (ref-16). First-purchase offer, second-purchase follow-up at two weeks, lapsed-buyer offer at three skipped weeks: a purpose-built ladder from trial to weekly habit.

## Closing
